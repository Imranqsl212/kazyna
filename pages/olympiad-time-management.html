Olympiad Strategy

Time Management for Economics Olympiads

Master the art of strategic time allocation to maximize your performance in economics competitions.

The 3-Phase Competition Strategy

Successful olympiad participants don't just know economics—they know how to manage their time under pressure. The key is dividing your competition time into three strategic phases that maximize both accuracy and coverage.

Phase 1: Quick Survey (10% of total time)

Before diving into any problem, spend the first 10% of your allocated time scanning the entire exam. This reconnaissance phase serves multiple purposes: identifying easy wins, spotting time-intensive problems, and mentally categorizing questions by difficulty and type.

During this phase, mark problems with a simple coding system: ★ for immediate confidence, ◆ for moderate difficulty, and ● for complex or time-consuming questions. This visual system helps you make strategic decisions throughout the exam.

Phase 2: Strategic Execution (75% of total time)

Begin with your ★ problems—these build confidence and secure guaranteed points early. The psychological benefit of early success cannot be overstated; it creates momentum and reduces anxiety for more challenging sections.

For calculation-heavy problems, always show your work clearly. Even if your final answer is incorrect, partial credit for proper methodology can be substantial. Use consistent notation and label your graphs clearly—judges appreciate organized thinking.

When tackling essay questions, spend 2-3 minutes outlining your response before writing. A clear structure with introduction, main arguments, and conclusion demonstrates economic reasoning skills that judges value highly.

Phase 3: Review and Optimization (15% of total time)

Reserve the final portion of your time for strategic review. Don't just check arithmetic— verify that your economic logic is sound. Ask yourself: "Does this answer make economic sense?" Many students lose points not from calculation errors, but from answers that violate basic economic principles.

Problem-Type Time Allocation

Different question types require different time investments. Understanding these patterns helps you allocate time more effectively during the competition.

Multiple Choice Questions (1-2 minutes each)

These should be your fastest point-earners. If you can't solve a multiple choice question within 2 minutes, mark your best guess and move on. The opportunity cost of spending 5 minutes on a single multiple choice question is too high.

Use elimination strategies: cross out obviously incorrect answers first, then apply economic reasoning to remaining options. Remember that extreme answers ("always," "never") are often incorrect in economics, where context matters greatly.

Short Answer Problems (5-8 minutes each)

These problems typically require calculations or brief explanations. Start by identifying what economic concept is being tested, then apply the appropriate formula or framework. Show your work step-by-step, as partial credit is common.

For graphical problems, draw large, clear diagrams with properly labeled axes. Use arrows to show shifts and clearly mark equilibrium points. A well-drawn graph can earn points even if your explanation has minor errors.

Essay Questions (15-25 minutes each)

Long-form responses require the most strategic time management. Spend 20% of your allocated time planning your response. Create a brief outline that includes your thesis, main supporting arguments, and conclusion.

Structure your essays with clear topic sentences and logical flow. Use specific economic terminology correctly—this demonstrates mastery to judges. Include relevant examples or case studies when possible, as they show applied understanding.

Stress Management During Competition

Time pressure creates stress, and stress impairs economic reasoning. Developing stress management techniques is crucial for optimal performance during competitions.

The Reset Technique

When you feel overwhelmed by a difficult problem, use the "reset technique": close your eyes, take three deep breaths, and mentally step back from the problem. Often, this brief pause allows you to see solutions that stress was blocking.

Strategic Skipping

Don't let pride keep you stuck on a single problem. If you've spent more than your allocated time on a question without progress, move on. Mark it clearly for return during your review phase. Sometimes, solving other problems provides insights that help with earlier difficulties.

Energy Management

Maintain steady energy throughout the competition. Bring water and light snacks if permitted. Take brief mental breaks between major sections—even 30 seconds of conscious breathing can restore focus and clarity.

Practice Strategies for Time Management

Effective time management skills develop through deliberate practice. Simply knowing these strategies isn't enough—you must train your ability to execute them under pressure.

Timed Practice Sessions

Regularly practice with strict time limits, even when studying individual topics. This builds your internal clock and helps you recognize how long different problem types actually take you to solve.

Use a timer for every practice session. Start with generous time limits and gradually reduce them as your speed improves. Track your performance to identify which problem types consistently take longer than expected.

Mock Competition Conditions

Simulate actual competition conditions during practice. Use the same type of calculator you'll have during the competition, practice in similar lighting conditions, and avoid external aids you won't have access to during the actual event.

Post-Practice Analysis

After each timed practice session, analyze not just which problems you got wrong, but how you allocated your time. Did you spend too long on low-value questions? Did you rush through high-value problems? This reflection builds strategic awareness.

Advanced Time Management Techniques

As you become more comfortable with basic time management, these advanced techniques can provide additional competitive advantages.

The Banking Strategy

When you complete problems faster than expected, "bank" that extra time for more challenging questions later. Keep a running mental tally of time saved versus time needed. This creates flexibility for unexpected difficulties.

Parallel Processing

For complex problems requiring multiple steps, work on different components simultaneously. While calculating one part, let your subconscious work on the conceptual framework for another section. This technique requires practice but can significantly improve efficiency.

Strategic Guessing

When time runs short, make educated guesses based on economic principles rather than random choices. Use your understanding of economic relationships to eliminate impossible answers and increase your probability of success.

Competition Day Timeline

Your time management begins before the competition starts. A well-planned competition day routine sets you up for optimal performance.

Pre-Competition Preparation

Arrive early to familiarize yourself with the testing environment. Bring backup materials (extra pencils, calculator batteries) to avoid time-wasting disruptions. Review your time allocation strategy one final time.

During the Competition

Stick to your planned time allocation but remain flexible. If you're ahead of schedule, use extra time for thorough review rather than rushing to the next section. If you're behind, identify which problems to skip rather than rushing through everything.

Final Minutes Strategy

In the last 5 minutes, focus on completing any partially finished problems rather than starting new ones. Ensure all answers are clearly marked and legible. A complete, clear answer is worth more than a brilliant but unfinished response.
