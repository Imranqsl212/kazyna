GDP explained: output, income, and the story behind a number.

GDP is one of the most quoted economic indicators, but in olympiad problems it is also one of the easiest to misuse. This guide gives you the clean mental model.

What GDP means

Gross domestic product is the market value of final goods and services produced within a country's borders during a specific period. Every word matters. Market value lets us add apples, apps, and airline tickets in money terms. Final goods prevent double counting. Domestic production focuses on location, not nationality.

If a Kyrgyz factory produces a machine in Bishkek, it counts in Kyrgyz GDP even if the owner lives abroad. If a Kyrgyz citizen earns income abroad, that belongs to national income concepts, not domestic production.

Three approaches

The expenditure approach is the one students memorize: Y = C + I + G + (X - M). The income approach adds wages, rent, interest, profits, and adjustments. The value-added approach adds the extra value created at each production stage.

Olympiad tip: when a question lists intermediate transactions, use value added. When it lists household, firm, government, and trade spending, use expenditure. When it lists factor payments, use income.

Real vs nominal GDP

Nominal GDP uses current prices. Real GDP holds prices constant to isolate output changes. If nominal GDP rises because prices rose, people are not necessarily producing more. This is why economists divide by a price index to compare across time.

Formula habit: write the units before calculating. If the price index is 125, divide by 125 and multiply by 100, not by 1.25 and then again by 100.

What GDP misses

GDP excludes unpaid care work, informal production, leisure, environmental depletion, and distribution. A country can have rising GDP with unequal gains or polluted cities. Strong answers respect GDP's usefulness while naming these blind spots.

Diagram and data example

Imagine consumption rises because households become confident. In AD-AS, aggregate demand shifts right. In the short run, real output and the price level rise. If the economy was below potential, output gains may be large. If it was near capacity, inflation pressure dominates.

Key takeaways

  • GDP measures domestic final production, not welfare.
  • Use expenditure, income, or value-added approach depending on the data.
  • Real GDP adjusts for price changes; nominal GDP does not.
  • In policy questions, connect GDP changes to employment, inflation, and capacity.
