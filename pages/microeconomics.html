Micro track

Microeconomics: incentives, choices, markets, and strategy.

Microeconomics is the language of marginal thinking. This page gives you the core models needed for essays, graphs, calculations, and olympiad puzzles.

Supply and demand

Demand slopes down because buyers usually want less at higher prices. Supply slopes up because higher prices make production more attractive. Equilibrium is where plans match. A price ceiling below equilibrium creates a shortage; a price floor above equilibrium creates a surplus.

Elasticity

Elasticity measures responsiveness. Olympiad questions often ask whether total revenue rises or falls after a price change. If demand is elastic, price and revenue move in opposite directions; if inelastic, they move together.

Utility and consumer behavior

Consumers allocate income until marginal utility per unit of money is equal across goods. The principle is not about perfect psychology; it is a clean model of trade-offs under constraints.

Production costs

Fixed costs do not change with output in the short run. Variable costs do. Marginal cost cuts average cost at its minimum. This one geometric fact explains many firm decisions.

Market structures

Perfect competition has many price takers. Monopoly restricts output where marginal revenue equals marginal cost. Monopolistic competition adds differentiation. Oligopoly requires strategic reasoning.

Game theory basics

Start with the payoff matrix. Check each player's best response for every rival action. A dominant strategy is best in all cases; Nash equilibrium is where no one wants to deviate alone.

Opportunity cost

Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative. It turns vague sacrifice into a measurable comparison and is the engine behind trade, specialization, and comparative advantage.

Comparative advantage

The producer with lower opportunity cost has comparative advantage. Even if one side is better at everything, both sides can gain from trade when relative costs differ.
